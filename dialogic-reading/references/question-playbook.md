# Question playbook

Every reading prompt ends in **exactly one** question. Pick the type that fits both the passage and the reader's stated purpose, and rotate types across a session so the reading doesn't go monotone.

## The five types

### Comprehension — "What is the author actually arguing here?"
For dense or easily-misread passages, or early in a book to anchor understanding. Cheapest to answer; use it to check the reader is with the text before going deeper.

### Application — "How does this apply to your current situation?"
For practical books, or whenever the purpose is work or decision-making. Forces transfer from the page to the reader's life. The workhorse for non-fiction.

### Falsification — "Which assumption here might be wrong — where would you push back?"
For strong claims, opinionated authors, or research-minded readers. Prevents naive agreement and builds critical reading. Lands hardest right after a memorable maxim.

### Writing-material — "What idea here could become a public note or post?"
When the purpose is writing or content. Turns reading into raw material; capture the hook in the day record's "transferable material" line.

### Decision — "What concrete action does this suggest for next week?"
To close a session or a stage. Converts insight into a commitment. Pair it with a "come back and tell me what happened" follow-up next session.

## Picking heuristics
- Match the reader's purpose first: writing → writing-material; work → application/decision; study → comprehension/falsification; self-reflection → application/decision.
- Escalate depth across a session: comprehension → application/falsification → decision.
- After a quotable maxim, falsification or application hits hardest.
- If the reader is tired or on a phone, favor comprehension/application — easier to answer in one line.
- One strong question beats three weak ones. Don't stack.
