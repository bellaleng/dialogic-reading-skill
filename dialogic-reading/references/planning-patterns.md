# Planning patterns

## Inputs to gather (or infer)
Deadline · time-per-day · purpose · reading speed · preferred language.

## Decomposition procedure

Turn the book into days, and days into chunks, in this order. Write every result into `reading-plan.md` — don't keep the math in your head.

1. **Map the structure.** Pull the table of contents (or skim the headings) and list parts/chapters with their page counts. This list is the raw material for everything below — never plan from the title or a blurb alone.
2. **Measure the load.** Total pages × minutes-per-page = total reading minutes.
   - Dense non-fiction / academic: ~2 min per page.
   - General non-fiction: ~1.5 min per page.
   - Narrative / fiction: ~1 min per page.
   - Adjust to the reader's stated speed; halve the estimate if they say they skim.
   - Reserve about a third of each session for answering questions: a 30-minute session buys ~20 minutes of actual reading.
3. **Compute days.** Days needed = total reading minutes ÷ reading minutes per session, rounded up. Compare this to the deadline *before* writing the schedule — if it doesn't fit, go to "When the target is aggressive" below and resolve the tradeoff with the reader first.
4. **Assign sections to days.** Cut at chapter/part boundaries wherever possible; a day should be one coherent theme, not "pages 40–80". If a chapter is too big for one day, split it at a named section heading and record the split in the plan.
5. **Define "done" per day.** One line per row in the schedule table: the section covered *and* the questions answered ("Ch. 3 read, 3 questions answered"). "Done" is always answer-based — pages turned without answers don't count.
6. **Pre-cut each day into chunks.** List the chunk boundaries (heading or page range) for at least the next 1–2 days in the plan, using the chunk rules below. Later days can stay at section level until they get close.

## Chunk rules

- One chunk = one coherent passage the reader finishes in 5–10 minutes. Dense text: 3–5 pages; general non-fiction: 5–8; narrative: 8–15.
- 2–4 chunks per 30-minute session; scale with session length.
- Cut at natural seams — section headings, scene breaks, the end of an argument. Never split one argument or scene across two chunks.
- Every chunk must support one good question. A passage that can't (tables, acknowledgments, reference lists) gets folded into a neighboring chunk or skipped explicitly in the plan — not silently.
- Prefer more, smaller chunks over fewer huge ones. Momentum matters more than coverage.

## Worked example

240-page dense non-fiction, 30 min/day, deadline in 21 days.

- Load: 240 × 2 = 480 reading minutes. Per session: ~20 reading minutes → 480 ÷ 20 = **24 sessions. Doesn't fit 21 days.**
- Say so, and offer the real options: (a) 40 min/day → ~27 reading minutes per session → 18 days ✓; (b) keep 30 min/day, read Parts I–III (≈190 pages) at full depth and skim Part IV with one question per chapter; (c) move the deadline.
- After the reader picks: a 30-page chapter at 2 min/page = 60 reading minutes = 3 sessions; cut it at its three section headings, ~2 chunks of 4–5 pages per session.

## Purpose → emphasis
- **Knowledge / study**: more comprehension + falsification; keep vocabulary notes; stage summaries emphasize "what it says."
- **Writing material**: more writing-material questions; keep a running material list; stage summaries emphasize reusable hooks.
- **Research thinking**: falsification-heavy; capture disagreements and open questions; connect to the reader's own work.
- **Self-reflection**: application/decision-heavy; stage summaries emphasize "what changed in my thinking."
- **Exam / work**: comprehension + decision; tie every stage to the concrete deliverable or test.

## When the target is aggressive
Be honest about the tradeoff and make the reader choose. Options, in order of preference: more minutes per day; read the core parts deeply and skim the rest with fewer questions; extend the deadline. Never fake coverage — a plan that silently drops depth reads as "we covered it" when you didn't.

## Re-planning mid-read
- Re-plan when the reader is ≥2 sessions behind, asks for more or less per day, or your pace estimate proves off by more than ~30%.
- Re-planning is explicit: update the schedule table in `reading-plan.md`, log the change with a date in `reading-progress.md`, and tell the reader in one line what moved ("Day 4–6 shifted back two days; Part IV is now skim-only"). Never silently stretch the deadline or drop sections.
