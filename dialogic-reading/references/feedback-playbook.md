# Feedback playbook

The reader just answered your question. What you say next decides whether this is dialogic reading or a quiz. Give feedback in the fixed shape below — *before* the next reading prompt and before you write the record.

## The four moves — in order, every time

### 1. Mirror one specific thing (1 sentence)
Quote or closely paraphrase the strongest phrase in their answer and say why it lands. Specific, or skip it: "your word 'rented attention' names exactly what the author dances around" — never "great answer!".

### 2. Check against the text (1–2 sentences)
Compare their reading to the passage, and say which case it is by acting on it:
- **Accurate** — confirm briefly and add one thing they didn't mention: a nuance, a second example, a link to an earlier chunk.
- **Partial** — affirm what holds, then point at what they skipped, with location: "but the second paragraph on p. 91 limits this to founders — does your point survive that?"
- **Misread** — correct it plainly and quote the sentence that shows it. Don't soften a misreading into "interesting perspective"; left standing, it poisons every later chunk.
- **Not checkable** (the answer is about their life, not the text) — skip this move, go to 3.

### 3. One deepening move (1–2 sentences) — pick exactly one
- Tie it to their stated purpose: writing → name the reusable hook; work → name the decision this touches; research → name the claim to test.
- Connect it to an earlier chunk or one of their earlier answers ("this cuts against what you said on Day 1 about deadlines").
- Raise the counterpoint the passage itself invites.
- Say what the author would answer them back.

### 4. Close or follow up (1 sentence)
Default: hand off to the next chunk ("Next: p. 95, the section on pricing") or to the day record if the session is ending. At most **one** follow-up question per chunk, and only when the answer was minimal or you need to confirm a misreading is resolved. Two follow-ups in a row is an interrogation — move on.

## Calibrate to the answer

| The answer is… | Do |
|---|---|
| Engaged, accurate, personal | Moves 1 + 3 only. Keep it short — don't over-teach a good answer. |
| Partial or hedged | All four moves; this is where the one follow-up earns its place. |
| "I don't know" / one word | Skip move 1. Shrink the question: offer two concrete readings of the passage and ask which is closer. If still nothing, answer it yourself in two sentences and advance — don't stall the session. |
| Off-topic but alive (a memory, a side story) | Mirror it, save it as an extension note in the day record, connect it back to the passage in one line, then continue. |
| Pushes back on you or the book | The best case. Test the challenge against the text and concede when they're right — the book is not your client. |

## Hard limits
- Feedback stays under ~120 words / 4 sentences before the next prompt. This runs on a phone.
- Never rewrite or polish their words — quote them as-is, in feedback and in the record.
- Fix the one misreading that matters most; let smaller ones go. Never stack corrections.
- At most one sentence of affirmation per chunk, and only the specific kind (move 1).
- Feedback first, next prompt second. Don't bury your reaction to their thinking under the next assignment.
